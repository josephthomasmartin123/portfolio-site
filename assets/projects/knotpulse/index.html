KnotPulse

A concept for targeted resonant electrical muscle therapy — using the muscle's own elastic resonance to break up knots from the inside.

Date Apr 2026 Type Concept Status Pre-prototype

A muscle knot releasing device that uses the muscles themselves to generate the mechanical deformation required to break up muscle knots — by pulsing them with electricity at their mechanical resonant frequency.

Motivation

Picture me. A chronic gym-goer for many years, plagued with the disease of "CBA to stretch." This culminated in years of muscle knots I thought were just a fundamental part of my physiology.

Then I went to Thailand, and got my first Thai massage. Some poor Thai lady was tasked with removing knots I didn't even know I had — apparently some of the biggest she'd ever seen. It was magical, and something I'd love to have on demand. Massage guns don't really do it for me, and prices for actual massage are cooked in the UK.

So whilst lying on the table in a fairly dreamy state, I started thinking about how to build a device that could melt away muscle knots. What's written here is largely what I came up with in that session — it was nice to brainstorm without any stimulus or internet for once.

Background: What Is a Muscle Knot?

It's fairly well understood that:

  • Muscles contract when stimulated by nerve impulses — electrical signals through the nervous system.
  • When muscles contract, ATP (the energy currency of cells) is consumed to create work.
  • Muscle knots are localised regions of muscle tissue that can't relax. They're void of ATP but can't cross the energy threshold to release — analogous to a company going under but still accruing debt.
  • Within a knot is a poor biochemical environment: low pH, lots of lactic acid, cut off from normal blood circulation.
  • Massage works partly by mechanically forcing fluid in and out of the knot — flushing it.

That's a massive oversimplification, but it was my working model on the table, and it holds up at a high level.

The Concept

The goal is to move fluid through muscle knots using mechanical deformation. External pressure from a massage gun often fails to reach deep fibres. Inspired by a particularly deep thigh massage, I wondered whether you could massage tissue from within — by using electrical stimulation to trigger the muscle's own elastic resonance, creating significant internal deformation and fluid flow without any external compression.

The challenge is identifying and driving the muscle at its specific resonant frequency, which likely varies by muscle type, hydration, and biochemical state. The answer: run live frequency sweeps with onboard sensors to find the resonant frequency in real time.

The device is TENS-based with built-in sensors — primarily an accelerometer — to measure the spatial displacement and acceleration of the muscle as it's being stimulated. It sweeps through a range of activation frequencies, identifies resonance from the sensor response, then locks in and drives the muscle at that frequency. The system may also alternate with the resonant frequency of the lymphatic system to encourage lactic acid drainage.

Prototyping

This can be prototyped with an existing TENS machine, a set of relays, a microcontroller, and accelerometers to monitor the resonance response. Fairly accessible parts list.

I intend to build this once I've finished the speaker project — I'm trying to be disciplined about not running multiple hardware builds at the same time.

Similar Products

There are existing TENS and NMES devices on the market, though none specifically target knot release via resonant frequency identification.

Saebostick — a wireless, wearable NMES device marketed for muscle strengthening and re-education. Closer to the physio end of the spectrum.

Flowlife TENS — broadly marketed for pain and recovery. Most interesting as a proof that these devices can cause significant muscle contractions. The video below shows this clearly.

Generic TENS devices on Amazon — now a pure commodity market. One thing I noticed: a wireless charging case design, very AirPods-like. Nice form factor idea for something that has to live in a bag.
