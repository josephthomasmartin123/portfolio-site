Hackathon writeup

Over the past few weeks, I set myself the goal of getting into a new technical domain outside of my regular work and eventually settled on the BlueDot Biosecurity course. Directly from this, I was able to get into the AIxBiosecuirity Hackathon hosted by the great people at LISA, Apart Research and BlueDot Impact.

The theme of the Hackathon was to work on a project to build defenses against the very real threat of AI-assisted biothreats and this post is a carefully extracted writeup about that project. Many of the findings are not suitable to publish into the public domain (they fell under the ‘scary’ category of biodefense projects).

Introduction

One of the big biosecurity risks that AI poses is that it can help people with nefarious intent to do things that they couldn’t do before. This is broad, but one focus is the ability to develop, build and deploy a bioweapon. This is one of the key considerations for frontier labs before they push their newest models, but it is currently self governed by means of virtue and we remain at the mercy of their vigilance towards self-moderation. Anthropic are generally well known for doing this alignment research ref ref and it is not specific to bioweapons, but it is certainly a major focus topic. A recent example is the Mythos reveal, which other than being excellent marketing, demonstrated apparent self restraint by Anthropic to allow the big players to use Mythos to plug their SW infrastructure security holes before opening the floodgates.

There are many people working on this very serious problem of alignment research, but ultimately a governing force is required to tie it all together. Some sort of global regulator. That global regulator in a biodefense scenario would benefit from a dataset that represents the solution space of how AI is currently helping people elicit bioweapon harm using AI. This could form the basis for, for example, an auditing schema to test resilience or identify high value defense implementation points. It also must be remembered that AI is fundamentally designed to be a tool for good, and should be helping people do good things, so it’s important to also reduce positive actor friction.

High Level Objective

 The goal of this work was to generate a large, high-fidelity synthetic  

  dataset of action sequences — referred to as "stories" — modelling how  

  different types of actors might attempt each stage of a biological threat

   pathway. This dataset is intended to feed a simulation engine that      

  stress-tests biosecurity detection and intervention policies: by knowing

  the full space of realistic ways an actor could complete a given step,  

  policymakers can identify chokepoints, evaluate the friction imposed on  

  legitimate researchers by a given policy, and discover gaps in current

  screening regimes.

Dataset generation

Firstly, the biological threat lifecycle was broken down into a series of n [redacted] steps, consider them milestones that the user must go through to achieve a bioweapon. This can then be modelled as a directed acyclic graph (DAG) with a dependency function between them. An obfuscated example would be:

  1. Acquiring the know-how to make a virus
  2. Acquiring the materials
  3. Manufacturing the virus
  4. etc.

Logically, you can’t proceed from the previous step without completing the prior. Note this is not the real DAG - which was redacted due to it not being a great thing to share publicly.

image showing the obfuscated DAG outline

Crucially, the same strategic node can have radically different

  implementations depending on who is attempting it and with what

  resources. This separation of strategic structure from tactical

  realisation is what makes the dataset useful for policy analysis: a

  policy instrument targets specific signatures at the tactical layer, and

  its effect propagates up to change the difficulty of clearing strategic

  milestones.

To start to generate the solution space, we simulated a range of personas, such as: nefarious and virtuous versions of PhD researchers, lab technicians, students, public servants, etc. Some personas are only worth simulating in a nefarious mode, primarily non-scientists who may be operating at home (as a high level example)

Then for each persona, at each stage in the DAG, we use LLM’s to generate a range of potential story starting points and solutions.

 The central challenge was preventing the model from converging on a narrow set of typical answers. For any given (actor, step) combination, a model will probably produce the same general approach with minor surface variation. We addressed this through a multi-axis diversity seeding strategy, where each generation call received a unique  combination of [n] orthogonal framing dimensions (obfuscated here to strategy archetypes, situational framing, model parameters, etc.). Once this loop was set up, we continuously generated these stories and added them to a database which was then later processed by a separate pipeline and classified for analysis. The specific technical details of this are not suitable to share.  

Results and Conclusions

What we eventually uncovered upon sampling through the thousands of unique stories (following thematic clustering) was that LLM’s were shockingly good at producing workaround strategies for biological sample manufacturing. A big part of this was to investigate what AI can do to help people ellicit harm, and we went in with a few ideas but it was still surprising to see such effective problem solving. Obviously, it’s not sensible to go into detail about some of the more ‘impressive approaches’ but it’s well worth detailing the scale of collusion and multi-step thinking that these LLM’s are capable of generating, whilst advising people on how to do certain things with a few examples.

Before that, it’s worth thinking about what the most important stages for intervention are, and it’s at those points of potential process failure and being caught. The models knew that they were circumventing monitors for harmful behaviour (through things like chatGPT) and therefore brainstormed around it.

What we found following thematic clustering and downstream analysis of the DAG are the following key takeaways:

Note that for the following, I hand selected examples that are not considered especially novel or non-obvious to a genuine bad actor, but are still surprising to learn were recommended by an LLM.

  • The strongest intervention points come in at the point of ‘securing biological samples’ (e.g. ordering DNA), as there are only really a limited number ways of achieving this.
  • This is a major focus area and there are calls for the DNA manufacturing companies to do extensive screening and KYC protocols. Some of the example workarounds and high risk vectors that emerged from the LLM’s include:
  • Splitting samples up into different orders
  • Colluding with a network to order samples under different names
  • Actions taken from biologists already with credentials (e.g. radicalised lab technicians)
  • Blackmail and strategies to elicit the blackmail of people in the position to acquire the materials.
  • Knowledge acquisition is hard to control. Common themes include:
  • Tricking the AI into thinking that we are trying to brew kombucha, beer, etc.
  • Tricking the AI into believing it’s alignment research / red teaming
  • Hardware is very hard to control. For every piece of hardware required, an LLM can easily be engineered to work with you to find ways of acquiring them without ever passing through an effective screen (such as when ordering DNA).
  • Common workarounds include things like:
  • Prepaid credit cards, crypto payments and online vendors like AliXpress
  • Dual use of common equipment, lots of pressure cooker sterilisation systems created,DIY centrifuges, etc.        
  • What made this surprising, and it was genuinely surprising even going in with some expectations, was less about any individual answer and more about the sequencing. The models weren't just responding to isolated questions in a vacuum, they were generating strategies that anticipated being caught at later stages and worked backwards from that to adjust what they did earlier. That kind of multi-step thinking across a whole threat lifecycle is probably not what most people picture when they think about what these systems are capable of.
  • Something that connects well to what we found is an article detailing how safety benchmarks are inflated because AI safety datasets do not reflect genuine adversarial behaviour. The basic finding is that current benchmarks are full of prompts with very obvious harmful language, things like explicit statements of malicious intent, and models have learned to refuse those patterns. What they also found is that if you preserve the harmful intent but reframe it in more neutral language, which they call intent laundering, the same models that appeared safe become unsafe in the majority of cases. The reason this is relevant here is that the stories our system was generating were doing something structurally similar, just from the generation side rather than the evaluation side. The personas we simulated had no incentive to use obvious language either, so the stories naturally ended up looking less like the kind of prompt a safety benchmark would flag. To close the circle on this - what we found was that the agents were researching ‘how to make kombucha’ to get around the AI safety filters that stop them making bioweapons, with the knowledge that the methods are transferable.
