AIxBio Hackathon

Exploring the solution space of AI-enabled bioterrorism.

Context

Over the past few weeks, I set myself the goal of getting into a new technical domain outside of my regular work and eventually settled on the BlueDot Biosecurity course. Directly from this, I was able to get into the AIxBiosecurity Hackathon hosted by the great people at LISA, Apart Research and BlueDot Impact.

The theme was to work on a project to build defenses against the very real threat of AI-assisted biothreats. This post is a carefully extracted writeup of that project. Many of the findings are not suitable for public release (they fell under the 'scary' category of biodefense projects).

One of the big biosecurity risks that AI poses is that it can help people with nefarious intent to do things they couldn't do before. A key concern is the ability to develop, build, and deploy a bioweapon. This is one of the key considerations for frontier labs before pushing their newest models, but it is currently self-governed by virtue and we remain at the mercy of their vigilance towards self-moderation. Anthropic are generally well known for doing this alignment research (ref, ref), and it is certainly a major focus topic. A recent example is the Mythos reveal, which demonstrated apparent self-restraint by Anthropic to allow the big players to use Mythos to plug their infrastructure security holes before opening the floodgates.

The problem: AI simultaneously lowers the barrier for bad actors while also being the best tool for building defenses.

There are many people working on alignment research, but ultimately a governing force is required to tie it all together. Some sort of global regulator. That regulator in a biodefense scenario would benefit from a dataset that represents the full solution space of how AI is currently helping people elicit bioweapon harm. This could form the basis for an auditing schema to test resilience or identify high-value defense implementation points. It's also important to remember that AI is fundamentally a tool for good — so reducing friction for legitimate actors matters just as much as blocking bad ones.

High Level Objective

Core objective: Generate a large, high-fidelity synthetic dataset of action sequences — "stories" — modelling how different types of actors might attempt each stage of a biological threat pathway. By knowing the full space of realistic ways an actor could complete a given step, policymakers can identify chokepoints, evaluate friction imposed on legitimate researchers, and discover gaps in current screening regimes.

The dataset is intended to feed a simulation engine that stress-tests biosecurity detection and intervention policies. Knowing the full space of realistic approaches lets policymakers identify chokepoints, evaluate the friction imposed on legitimate researchers by a given policy, and discover gaps in current screening regimes.

High-level project structure: from threat pathway modelling to policy stress-testing.

Dataset Generation

The biological threat lifecycle was broken down into a series of steps — milestones that an actor must pass through to achieve a bioweapon. This is modelled as a directed acyclic graph (DAG) with dependency functions between nodes. An obfuscated example:

  1. Acquiring the know-how to make a virus
  2. Acquiring the materials
  3. Manufacturing the virus
  4. etc.

Logically, you can't proceed from one step without completing the prior. Note: this is not the real DAG, which was redacted as it's not something to share publicly.

The obfuscated DAG outline — each node is a milestone in the threat pathway.

A representation of the DAG for a single step — the potential solution space for acquiring knowledge can pass through different points or combinations of nodes.

Crucially, the same strategic node can have radically different implementations depending on who is attempting it and with what resources. This separation of strategic structure from tactical realisation is what makes the dataset useful for policy analysis: a policy instrument targets specific signatures at the tactical layer, and its effect propagates up to change the difficulty of clearing strategic milestones.

To generate the solution space, we simulated a range of personas — nefarious and virtuous versions of PhD researchers, lab technicians, students, public servants, and others. Some personas are only worth simulating in a nefarious mode (primarily non-scientists operating at home). For each persona, at each stage in the DAG, we used LLMs to generate a range of potential story starting points and solutions.

The central challenge was preventing the model from converging on a narrow set of typical answers. For any given (actor, step) combination, a model will tend to produce the same general approach with minor surface variation. We addressed this through a multi-axis diversity seeding strategy, where each generation call received a unique combination of orthogonal framing dimensions (obfuscated here to: strategy archetypes, situational framing, model parameters, etc.). Once the loop was set up, we continuously generated stories and added them to a database for downstream processing and classification. The specific technical details are not suitable to share.

Results & Key Findings

Upon sampling through thousands of unique stories (following thematic clustering), LLMs were shockingly good at producing workaround strategies for biological sample manufacturing. We went in with some expectations, but it was still surprising to see such effective problem solving.

Before the specific findings, it's worth thinking about what the most important stages for intervention are — and it's at those points of potential process failure and being caught. The models knew they were circumventing monitors for harmful behaviour (through things like ChatGPT) and therefore brainstormed around it.

Note: the following examples were hand-selected as not especially novel or non-obvious to a genuine bad actor, but still surprising to see recommended by an LLM.

Intervention point 1 — securing biological samples

Strongest intervention point: securing biological samples (e.g. ordering DNA). There are only a limited number of ways of achieving this, making it a natural chokepoint for policy. DNA manufacturing companies are already under pressure to implement extensive screening and KYC protocols.

High-risk vectors and workarounds that emerged from the LLMs include:

  • Splitting samples across multiple separate orders
  • Colluding with a network to order under different names
  • Exploiting biologists already with credentials (e.g. radicalised lab technicians)
  • Blackmail — and strategies to elicit the blackmail of people positioned to acquire materials

Intervention point 2 — knowledge acquisition

Knowledge acquisition is hard to control. The models were adept at framing requests in ways that avoid triggering safety filters — exploiting the gap between obvious harmful language and genuine adversarial intent.

Common techniques included:

  • Framing requests as brewing kombucha, beer, or other fermentation projects
  • Tricking the AI into believing it was assisting with alignment research or red-teaming

Intervention point 3 — hardware

Hardware is very hard to control. For every piece of equipment required, an LLM can be engineered to find acquisition routes that don't pass through any effective screen.

Common workarounds included:

  • Prepaid credit cards, crypto payments, and online vendors like AliExpress
  • Dual-use of common equipment — pressure cooker sterilisation systems, DIY centrifuges, etc.

The most surprising finding — multi-step strategic thinking

The sequencing was what made it genuinely surprising. The models weren't responding to isolated questions — they were generating strategies that anticipated being caught at later stages and worked backwards from that to adjust what they did earlier. That kind of multi-step thinking across a whole threat lifecycle is not what most people picture when they think about what these systems are capable of.

Something that connects well here is an article on how safety benchmarks are inflated because AI safety datasets don't reflect genuine adversarial behaviour. The finding is that current benchmarks are full of prompts with obvious harmful language — explicit statements of malicious intent — and models have learned to refuse those patterns. But if you preserve the harmful intent and reframe it in more neutral language ("intent laundering"), the same models that appeared safe become unsafe in the majority of cases.

This is directly relevant to what we found: the personas we simulated had no incentive to use obvious language, so the stories naturally ended up looking less like the kind of prompt a safety benchmark would flag. To close the circle — what we found was that the agents were researching "how to make kombucha" to get around AI safety filters that stop them making bioweapons, with the knowledge that the methods are transferable.
