3D-Printed 3-Way Speaker System

A fully active stereo speaker system built from scratch in a London flat — 3D printed, plaster-loaded cabinets driven by DSP amplifier boards.

Status Operational — tuned, playing to 40Hz Amplifier JAB5 (2× boards, cascade) Workhorse Bambu A1 Mini (180mm³)

After finishing the Nixie speaker project I wanted something with more low-end output — but it had to be manufacturable in a small London flat with minimal tools. The constraint shaped everything: no CNC, no large workshop, just a desktop 3D printer and whatever could be sourced or improvised.

Concept

I started with a few rough concepts. The first was an omnidirectional design — interesting acoustically, but ruled out immediately: omnidirectional speakers are great, unless you put them up against a wall, which is generally the best place to put a speaker.

The second concept was more promising: a 3-way design with a 3D-printed shell that could be infilled with plaster of paris for mass loading, with a bent bass reflex port for low-end extension and wire channels routed through the structure. This was interesting enough to prototype.

V1 — First prototype

The manufacturing approach was simple in principle: 3D print a set of interlocking shells, bond them together, and fill with a gypsum and PVA mixture for mass loading. The electronics choice was the JAB5 amplifier board — integrated DSP, signal processing, 4×100W channels, Bluetooth, and daisy-chainable for an 8×100W cascade system.

Filling with liquid plaster from the top turned out to be a much bigger challenge than anticipated. Getting the viscosity right is a non-trivial problem: too thin and it leaks through every gap; too thick and it blocks the flow channels. Gypsum also thickens during pouring, making the whole process a nonlinear nightmare. The leak below was noticed early — it got considerably worse as the reservoir of plaster gradually found every exit.

V2 — Redesign

V1 taught several clear lessons. The continuous-wall design meant that a single leak could compromise the whole build, and the interconnected fill channels made viscosity management extremely difficult. The screw-free assembly using epoxy was expensive and not very effective.

V2 addressed all of this. Shells became modular — each infilled individually before assembly, so a failed pour means replacing one shell rather than starting over. Height was reduced by incorporating the bass reflex port as one of the structural walls. Angled screw mounts replaced epoxy, providing proper clamping force between pieces.

Wall construction

Each wall is a multilayer composite. From inside to outside: acoustic foam liner, 3D-printed shell, gypsum/PVA infill, vinyl wrap as the outer airtight envelope.

The build sequence for each shell:

  1. Print shells
  2. Mix gypsum and PVA with water, pour into individual shell, allow to dry
  3. Bond infilled shells together with screws and Stixall sealing adhesive
  4. Stick acoustic foam liner to inside wall
  5. Vinyl wrap the outside — airtight envelope and structural tension layer
  6. Stuff with wool
  7. Exterior finishing to follow

Stixall handles air sealing, adhesion, and gap filling between shells. The vinyl wrap is the primary airtight barrier and also adds a tensioned outer face that stiffens the whole assembly. Screws ensure rigidity during cure and prevent shells separating when the speaker is handled — a failure mode observed with V1, where the gypsum (unsurprisingly) had no tensile strength.

Build

Vinyl wrapping provided an airtight seal and covered the rough plaster fills, though it was not intended as the final aesthetic — that comes later. Getting an even wrap on an irregular multi-part 3D-printed surface was genuinely difficult. The conclusion: a future career in vinyl wrapping is firmly off the table.

Electronics

Programming the JAB5 boards was done in Sigma Studio. Getting the PC to recognise the programming board required a few hours of troubleshooting — the board itself needed reprogramming first. Once connected, dialling in the DSP filters was straightforward and the system sounded good early on.

The original plan was a JAB5 board mounted to the back of each speaker, linked over I²S in master-slave configuration. The discovery that I²S cables max out at around 10cm was an unexpected blocker — not ideal for a stereo pair. The solution: mount both boards on one speaker and run speaker-level cables to the other, making it a passive satellite. Less elegant, but it works well and keeps the cascade system intact.

Aesthetics — concepts

With the system functional, attention turned to how the speakers should look in their final form. The vinyl-wrapped boxes were never going to be permanent. Using a reference photo of the two wrapped speakers, I used AI image generation to explore a range of finishing concepts — parametric 3D-printed panels, block patterns, and print-based surface treatments.

Current status

Both speakers are tuned and fully operational. The cascade system is implemented — two JAB5 boards running in master-slave configuration, 8×100W total, with the system tuned down to 40Hz. For a pair of 3D-printed boxes built on a kitchen table, the output is significant.

Next steps, when time allows:

  • Acoustic measurement and calibration (REW + measurement mic)
  • Exterior finishing — vinyl wrap or 3D-printed parametric panels bonded to the outside
